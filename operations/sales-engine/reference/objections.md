# Objection Handling by Persona

> Persona-calibrated responses to common objections. Load on-demand when
> preparing for calls or responding to prospect pushback.

## Framework: LAER

Every objection response follows LAER:
1. **L**isten -- acknowledge what they said (shows respect)
2. **A**cknowledge -- validate their concern (builds trust)
3. **E**xplore -- ask a question to understand the root (finds the real objection)
4. **R**espond -- address the actual concern with evidence (resolves it)

Never skip steps 1-3. The first objection stated is rarely the real objection.

## Objection: "It's too expensive"

### For C-Suite
**Root cause**: They don't see the ROI justification.
> "I hear you -- budget matters, especially when you're allocating across competing priorities.
> Can I ask: what would it be worth to solve {their specific pain}? If the number we discussed --
> {their stated cost of the problem} -- is accurate, we're looking at a {X}x return in year one.
> What would make this an easy yes from a budget perspective?"

### For VP/Director
**Root cause**: They need ammunition to justify upward.
> "Totally fair. Let me help you build the business case. When we worked with {similar company},
> they saw {specific metric improvement} within {timeframe}. Would a one-page ROI projection
> with your specific numbers help you make the case internally?"

### For Technical
**Root cause**: They're comparing to open source or build-vs-buy.
> "What are you comparing against? If it's build-in-house, I'd want to understand the team
> cost and timeline. Most teams we talk to estimate 6-9 months for a comparable build, which
> at loaded engineering cost is ${calculation}. We get you live in {weeks}."

### For Procurement
**Root cause**: They're benchmarking or have a target price.
> "I want to make sure we find the right package. Can you share the budget range you're
> working within? Sometimes we can adjust scope or payment terms to fit the procurement cycle."

## Objection: "We already have a solution"

### For C-Suite
> "Smart to have something in place. The reason similar companies come to us is usually not
> to replace what works, but to fill a gap they've discovered at scale. What's the one thing
> you wish your current solution did better?"

### For VP/Director
> "Totally fair. How's it working for {specific use case they mentioned}? The teams we work
> with usually have a solution for the 80% but struggle with {our differentiator area}."

### For Technical
> "Respect for having something running. What's the maintenance burden look like? Job postings
> suggest you're hiring for {relevant role} -- is that related to scaling the current system?"

---

## Objection: "Bad timing"

### For C-Suite
> "Understood. When does your fiscal planning cycle start? I want to make sure we're in the
> conversation at the right time. In the meantime, would a benchmark report on what similar
> companies are doing be useful?"

### For VP/Director
> "When would be better? Happy to reconnect then. One thing -- {trigger event we identified}
> suggests this might become more urgent soon. Worth a 20-minute check-in to see if the
> timeline has shifted?"

### For Technical
> "No rush. Can I share a technical deep-dive doc so you have context when timing is right?
> If anything changes with {their pain point}, I'm a quick email away."

## Objection: "We need to think about it"

### For All Personas
**This is not a real objection. It's a signal that something is unresolved.**

> "Absolutely take the time you need. Can I ask -- what specifically needs more thought?
> Is it the fit, the pricing, the timing, or something else? I want to make sure I've
> given you everything you need to make a confident decision."

**If they can't articulate it**: The real objection is hidden. Likely one of:
- They need to consult someone else (Economic Buyer not identified)
- They're comparing to a competitor (Competition unknown)
- They don't have budget authority (Decision Process not mapped)
- They're not actually in pain (Pain not acute enough)

Ask: "If it were entirely up to you, would you move forward?"
- Yes -> "Who else needs to weigh in?" (find the real decision process)
- No -> "What's the hesitation?" (find the real objection)

## Objection: "Can you send me a proposal?"

### Before Discovery
> "Happy to -- but I want to make sure I send you something relevant, not a generic deck.
> Can I ask 3 quick questions so I tailor it to your situation?"

(This is a deflection tactic. They want you to go away. The questions keep the conversation going.)

### After Discovery, Before Demo
> "Absolutely. Before I do, I want to make sure the proposal addresses your top priorities.
> You mentioned {pain} and {criteria}. Are those still the top 2? And who else will review
> the proposal with you?"

### After Demo
> "I'll have that to you by {specific date}. Before I build it -- the last thing I want is
> for the proposal to miss the mark. Can we confirm: the three things you're evaluating on
> are {criterion 1}, {criterion 2}, and {criterion 3}. Anything I'm missing?"

---

## Objection: "Your competitor has feature X"

### Framework: Acknowledge, Reframe, Differentiate

> "You're right, they do. Here's what I'd encourage you to evaluate: {feature X} solves
> {narrow problem}. What we've found is that {broader problem} is usually the real challenge.
> When {similar company} evaluated both, they chose us because {differentiator}. Would it
> help to see that in a side-by-side comparison?"

**Key**: Never trash the competitor. Acknowledge their strength, then reframe the evaluation
criteria to where we win.

---

## Objection: "We're going to build it in-house"

### For C-Suite
> "That's an option. The companies we work with who went that route typically spent
> {X months} and {$Y} in engineering time before reaching feature parity with what we
> deliver out of the box. The question is whether engineering bandwidth is better spent
> on your core product or on this infrastructure."

### For Technical
> "I respect the build instinct. What's the realistic timeline and team allocation?
> Most teams underestimate the maintenance burden -- it's not the v1 that kills you,
> it's the ongoing 20% of an engineer's time keeping it running. Want me to share what
> the typical build-vs-buy calculation looks like for a company your size?"

---

## Meta-Rules for Objection Handling

1. **Never argue**. Acknowledge, explore, then address.
2. **The stated objection is rarely the real one**. Always explore before responding.
3. **Use their words back to them**. Shows you listened.
4. **End with a question**. Keep the conversation going.
5. **Have one relevant proof point ready** for every objection. Data beats opinion.
6. **If you don't know the answer**: "That's a fair question. Let me get you the
   specific answer rather than guess. Can I follow up by {date}?"
