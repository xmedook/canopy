# Department Manifest Format

> The standard for defining departments within a division. Departments are
> the coordination layer between divisions and teams — they group related teams
> under a shared mission, establish a department-level budget envelope, and define
> the cross-team patterns that govern how teams work together within the division.

---

## Why Departments

Teams coordinate agents. Divisions coordinate the entire operation. Departments
fill the gap between these two layers.

Without departments, a division containing 8 teams has no intermediate authority
for budget allocation, no named owner for cross-team coordination, and no clear
escalation target short of the division head. Every cross-team dispute routes
directly to the top of the division — a Shannon bandwidth violation at the
division level.

Departments solve this by applying **Ashby's Law of Requisite Variety** at the
mid-level: a department head absorbs the variety generated by 2–6 related teams,
translating it into a smaller set of signals before they reach the division.
This is not bureaucracy — it is variety management. The division can only govern
what it can process. Departments increase the operation's governable surface.

A department is a named group of teams that:

- Shares a **budget ceiling** distinct from individual team budgets
- Operates under a **common mission** that scopes their collective work
- Has a **department head** who is accountable for cross-team output quality
- Defines **cross-team coordination patterns** for work that spans team boundaries
- Follows **escalation rules** for situations that exceed departmental authority
- Reports to a **division** as its parent unit

Without departments, division heads become the single point of failure for all
mid-level coordination. With departments, variety is managed at the correct
altitude — close enough to the work to be informed, far enough to maintain
strategic perspective.

## File Convention

Every department definition lives in `departments/{division-id}/{department-id}.md`.
Like team files, it follows a YAML frontmatter + Markdown body convention. The
frontmatter is machine-readable. The body defines the department's operating model.

```
{operation-root}/
├── company.yaml
├── agents/
│   └── {agent-id}.md
├── teams/
│   └── {team-id}.md
└── departments/
    └── {division-id}/
        ├── software-engineering.md
        ├── data-platform.md
        └── infrastructure.md
```

The division subdirectory keeps department files scoped to their parent. A
department in `product` and a department in `engineering` may share similar
names but are distinct entities with separate governance.

## Frontmatter Schema

```yaml
---
name: string              # Display name ("Software Engineering")
id: string                # URL-safe slug ("software-engineering")
division: string          # Parent division id ("engineering")
head: string              # Agent id who heads this department
teams: [string]           # List of team ids grouped under this department
budget: number            # Department-level budget ceiling (USD/month)
signal: string            # S=(M,G,T,F,W) for department-level outputs
---
```

### Field Reference

| Field | Type | Required | Description |
|-------|------|----------|-------------|
| `name` | string | yes | Human-readable department name |
| `id` | string | yes | URL-safe identifier matching the filename |
| `division` | string | yes | Parent division id; must reference a valid division |
| `head` | string | yes | Agent id who heads this department; must exist in `agents/` |
| `teams` | list | yes | Team ids grouped under this department; all must exist in `teams/` |
| `budget` | number | yes | Monthly USD ceiling for the department as a whole |
| `signal` | string | yes | Signal Theory default encoding for department-level outputs |

### Signal Encoding

The `signal` field follows the same 5-dimension format as agent and team definitions:

```yaml
signal: S=(mode, genre, type, format, structure)
```

For departments, this encodes the expected output mode for department-level status
reports, resource requests, cross-team coordination signals, and escalations to
the division. Examples:

```yaml
# Software Engineering department producing weekly status reports
signal: S=(data, report, inform, markdown, department-status)

# Data Platform department producing resource allocation requests
signal: S=(data, brief, direct, markdown, resource-request)

# Infrastructure department producing incident summaries for the division
signal: S=(linguistic, report, inform, markdown, incident-summary)
```

### Budget Semantics

Department budget is a ceiling on the **sum** of all team spending within the
period, not a per-team cap. Individual teams retain their own `budget` fields.
The department budget adds a rollup layer between team budgets and the division:

```
Team level:        frontend-platform team   ≤ $2,000/month
                   backend-services team    ≤ $3,000/month
                   api-gateway team         ≤ $1,500/month
                   developer-experience team≤ $1,500/month

Department ceiling: software-engineering    ≤ $6,000/month
```

The more restrictive limit applies. If three teams have collectively spent
$5,800 of the department's $6,000 ceiling, the remaining $200 is the effective
cap for all further team spending in the period — regardless of what each
team's individual ceiling says.

Budget enforcement checks proceed in order:

1. **Agent-level check**: Does the individual agent have budget remaining?
2. **Team-level check**: Does the team have collective budget remaining?
3. **Department-level check**: Does the department have budget remaining?
4. **Division-level check**: Does the division have budget remaining?
5. **Company-level check**: Does the operation have monthly budget remaining?

All five checks must pass. The first failure blocks execution and triggers the
relevant enforcement tier.

## Body Sections

The Markdown body MUST contain these 4 sections in order:

### 1. Mission

What this department exists to do. Written as a purpose statement, not a task
list. One to three sentences.

```markdown
# Mission

[Department name] exists to [primary purpose]. We are accountable for
[outcome domain] and measure success by [quality signal].
```

### 2. Teams Overview

A brief description of each team in the department and the scope it owns.
This section makes the department's internal structure legible to anyone
reading the file — without requiring them to open each team file.

```markdown
# Teams Overview

## [team-name]
[One to two sentences describing what this team owns and produces.]

## [team-name]
[One to two sentences describing what this team owns and produces.]
```

### 3. Cross-Team Coordination

How teams within the department collaborate on work that spans team boundaries.
This section documents the repeating patterns that govern cross-team handoffs,
shared deliverables, and joint decision points. Use numbered steps for
sequential flows.

```markdown
# Cross-Team Coordination

## Pattern: [Name]
**Trigger**: [what starts this pattern]
**Teams involved**: [team ids]
**Flow**:
1. [team-id] produces [output] and delivers it to [team-id]
2. [team-id] integrates and returns [output] for review
3. Department head approves final output before division delivery
```

### 4. Escalation Rules

Conditions that require the department head to act, or that escalate beyond
the department to the division. Be specific — vague escalation rules produce
inconsistent behavior.

```markdown
# Escalation Rules

- ESCALATE TO DEPARTMENT HEAD when: [specific condition]
- ESCALATE TO DIVISION when: [specific condition requiring division-level involvement]
- BLOCK AND WAIT FOR HUMAN when: [irreversible action with ambiguous authorization]
```

## Relationship to Divisions and Teams

Departments sit at the **middle layer** of the three-tier organizational hierarchy:

```
Division (division-format.md)
  └── Department (department-format.md)
        └── Team (team-format.md)
              └── Agent (agent-format.md)
```

Divisions set strategic direction and hold the top-level budget envelope.
Teams execute tasks and coordinate individual agents. Departments bridge the two:

- **Downward**: A department allocates budget across its teams, sets cross-team
  coordination patterns, and owns the interfaces between teams.
- **Upward**: A department aggregates team-level signals into department-level
  status, absorbs cross-team variety before it reaches the division, and
  surfaces escalations only when they exceed departmental authority.

```
Division head receives:      Department-level status report (once per cycle)
                             Escalations that exceed department authority

Department head receives:    Team-level status reports (once per cycle)
                             Cross-team blockers
                             Budget alerts from any team

Team manager receives:       Agent task completions and blockers
                             Cross-team handoffs directed at the team
```

An agent belongs to exactly one team. A team belongs to exactly one department.
A department belongs to exactly one division. This strict hierarchy ensures
every entity has a single, unambiguous escalation path.

## Validation Rules

A department file is valid if:

1. YAML frontmatter parses without error
2. All required frontmatter fields are present
3. `division` references a valid division id
4. `head` is a valid agent id that exists in `agents/`
5. All `teams` are valid team ids that exist in `teams/`
6. The `head` agent belongs to one of the department's `teams`
7. `budget` is greater than 0
8. `budget` is less than or equal to the parent division's budget
9. `signal` field has exactly 5 comma-separated dimensions
10. All 4 body sections are present (matched by `# Section Name` headers)
11. No team appears in more than one department's `teams` list within the
    same division
12. The `id` field matches the filename (without the `.md` extension)
13. The file lives under `departments/{division-id}/` matching the `division` field

## Example

```yaml
---
name: Software Engineering
id: software-engineering
division: engineering
head: vp-engineering
teams:
  - frontend-platform
  - backend-services
  - api-gateway
  - developer-experience
budget: 6000
signal: S=(data, report, inform, markdown, department-status)
---
```

```markdown
# Mission

Software Engineering exists to deliver production-quality software across
the full application stack. We are accountable for code quality, system
reliability, and cross-team delivery velocity. We measure success by
defect escape rate, cross-team handoff latency, and deployment frequency.

# Teams Overview

## frontend-platform
Owns all client-facing UI surfaces, the component library, and frontend build
infrastructure. Produces production builds consumed by the api-gateway team.

## backend-services
Owns core business logic, domain models, and internal service APIs. Primary
consumer of api-gateway contracts and primary producer of data consumed by
frontend-platform.

## api-gateway
Owns the public API surface: routing, authentication middleware, rate limiting,
and the OpenAPI specification. The interface contract between external clients
and backend-services.

## developer-experience
Owns CI/CD pipelines, developer tooling, local environment setup, and internal
documentation. Enables all other teams to ship with lower friction.

# Cross-Team Coordination

## Pattern: Feature Delivery
**Trigger**: A task with `type: feature` and `scope: cross-team` enters `status: active`
**Teams involved**: backend-services, api-gateway, frontend-platform
**Flow**:
1. `backend-services` produces domain logic and internal API; delivers
   a service contract document to `api-gateway`
2. `api-gateway` exposes the route, updates the OpenAPI spec, and delivers
   an endpoint specification to `frontend-platform`
3. `frontend-platform` implements the UI and delivers integration test results
   to `api-gateway` for contract verification
4. `api-gateway` confirms end-to-end contract compliance and notifies
   department head
5. Department head reviews cross-team signal scores before approving for
   division-level status update

## Pattern: Developer Tooling Rollout
**Trigger**: `developer-experience` ships a breaking change to CI/CD or local dev setup
**Teams involved**: developer-experience, all affected teams
**Flow**:
1. `developer-experience` publishes a migration guide and announces in
   department coordination channel
2. Each affected team designates one member to validate the change in their
   context and reports PASS or FAIL within 24 hours
3. On all PASS: `developer-experience` promotes to department-wide rollout
4. On any FAIL: `developer-experience` holds rollout and escalates to
   department head for prioritization

# Escalation Rules

- ESCALATE TO DEPARTMENT HEAD when: A cross-team handoff has been blocked for
  more than 24 hours with no resolution path
- ESCALATE TO DEPARTMENT HEAD when: Any team's budget is within 20% of
  exhaustion before the mid-point of the period
- ESCALATE TO DEPARTMENT HEAD when: An API contract change would break an
  existing consumer outside the department
- ESCALATE TO DIVISION when: Department budget is within 15% of exhaustion
- ESCALATE TO DIVISION when: A delivery decision requires resources or authority
  beyond the department's scope
- ESCALATE TO DIVISION when: An architectural decision affects another department
  within the division or a department in another division
- BLOCK AND WAIT FOR HUMAN when: Any change targets production data schemas
  without a reviewed and approved migration plan
```

## Common Mistakes

**Wrong: A department head who does not belong to any team in the department.**
The head must be grounded in one of the department's teams. Heads who float
above the work lose signal fidelity.

**Wrong: Treating the department budget as the sum of team budgets.**
The department ceiling must be lower than the sum of team ceilings to provide
meaningful control. A ceiling equal to or higher than the sum is unenforced and
produces false budget confidence.

**Wrong: Assigning a team to two departments.**
Teams have a single departmental context. Cross-department work flows via
defined coordination patterns and handoffs — not dual membership.

**Wrong: Vague cross-team patterns like "teams will coordinate as needed."**
Coordination patterns must name specific triggers, the teams involved, and the
flow of work. Agents cannot coordinate on ambiguous instructions.

**Wrong: Routing all cross-team signals directly to the division.**
The department head exists precisely to absorb mid-level variety. Escalating
to the division for issues that can be resolved at the department level is an
Ashby violation — it overloads the division's coordination channel.

**Wrong: Setting department budget lower than any single team's budget.**
If one team's ceiling exceeds the department ceiling, the department ceiling
is the only limit that applies — the team ceiling becomes unreachable.
Ensure team ceilings are individually consistent with the department ceiling.

### Departments as Mid-Level Viable Systems

In Signal Theory, a department is a **mid-level cluster** in the Signal Network —
a group of team clusters (subnets) with related genre competence, unified under
a shared mission and a single coordination interface toward the division.

| DEPARTMENT.md Element | Signal Network Function |
|----------------------|------------------------|
| Teams list | Subnets grouped into a cluster — each team is a tightly-coupled subnet; the department is the supercluster |
| Department mission | Shared genre competence at the cluster level — what classes of signals this supercluster handles |
| Department head | Cluster gateway to the division — the node that translates team-level signals into department-level signals |
| Cross-team patterns | System 2 (Coordination) at the department scale — how S1 clusters synchronize across team boundaries |
| Escalation rules | Algedonic channel — viability-preserving bypass when normal cross-team coordination fails |
| Budget ceiling | Aggregate channel capacity at the department level — Shannon constraint on the supercluster |

**Recursive viability**: Every department IS a viable system. It has its own
S1 (teams executing work), S2 (cross-team coordination patterns), S3 (department
head allocating budget and resolving cross-team blockers), S4 (department-level
pattern recognition surfaced in status reports), and S5 (department mission).
This is Beer's recursion applied one level above the team: the same 5-subsystem
structure repeats at the department scale exactly as it repeats at the team scale.

## Signal Theory Position

This spec implements **Layer 1 (Network clusters) + Layer 7 (Governance, recursive VSM)**
of the Optimal System architecture — at the intermediate scale between team clusters
and division nodes.

Departments ARE mid-level superclusters in the Signal Network. Where a team
encodes a single subnet (tightly-coupled agents sharing genre competence), a
department encodes a supercluster of subnets (related teams sharing a mission
envelope). The department format applies the same structural logic as the team
format, one tier up:

| VSM Subsystem | Department Format Element |
|---------------|--------------------------|
| S1 — Operations | `teams` — the teams doing the work |
| S2 — Coordination | Cross-Team Coordination section — synchronization between teams |
| S3 — Control | `head` field — accountable for cross-team output quality and budget |
| S4 — Intelligence | Escalation Rules section — what the department observes and surfaces to the division |
| S5 — Policy | Department Mission section — the identity statement that bounds team behavior |

**Most relevant governing principles:**
- **Beer (recursion)** — Every department replicates the 5-subsystem structure.
  A viable department at any scale follows the same pattern as a viable team.
- **Ashby (variety management)** — The department head must have sufficient variety
  (authority, context, coordination tools) to absorb the variety generated by all
  teams in the department before it reaches the division. An under-resourced
  department head is an Ashby violation at the mid-level.
- **Shannon (bandwidth)** — The department is a compression layer. It reduces the
  number of signals the division must process by aggregating team-level status into
  department-level status. A department that routes all team signals directly to
  the division is a Shannon bandwidth violation.

See `architecture/optimal-system-mapping.md` for the canonical layer mapping.
